Impetus

Pedagogy

These principles have guided me in the design of this tutorial, and I hope that they will help you understand the meta-project and thus accelerate your learning:

  • Programming is best learnt by doing. As such, a high proportion of the material consists of exercises, and struggling with them should occupy most of your time.
  • Projects should be realistic and substantial. This helps retention by situating the new knowledge in a meaningful context. To this end, I have picked a meaty dataset with real-world implications and designed exercises to draw these out.
  • Tutorials should be beautiful. Many of the resources that we love, like Learn You A Haskell for Great Good! and Beautiful Racket are beautiful, and I don't think it's a coincidence.

    • Finally, let's clarify our goal. It isn't to know the rules for how to use WITH, or when to pick a LEFT JOIN over an INNER JOIN. We know we have been successful if after writing a SQL query, you can close your eyes and imagine what the computer would do and what output it would give. It is this mental model that we are after.

    Dataset

    The dataset we will examine documents Texas death row inmates executed between 1976, when the Supreme Court reinstated the death penalty, and 2017. On one level, it is simply a part of a mundane programming tutorial. On another, each row represents immense suffering, lives lost, and in some cases redemption and acceptance.

    In preparing for this dataset, I was deeply moved by a number of the statements and found myself re-evaluting my position on capital punishment. I hope that as we examine the data, you too will contemplate the deeper issues at play.

    The designers at Quora, where I used to work, often talked about the system-view versus the story-view. This dataset happens to be strong at both. I hope that the exercises will bring out the incredible stories behind some of the cases, as well as patterns that aid our understanding of capital punishment in Texas.
